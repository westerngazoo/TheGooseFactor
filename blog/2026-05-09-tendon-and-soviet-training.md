# Two Training Traditions Everyone Should Know

Most lifters learn one tradition — the one their first coach used —
and stop. That's a mistake. Two methodologies in particular have
produced disproportionate amounts of useful evidence and aren't
in most gym-bro curricula:

1. **Tendon resilience training** — the KOT (Knees Over Toes) /
   Ben Patrick school + classical heavy-slow-resistance (HSR)
   tendon-rehabilitation research.
2. **Soviet conjugate / Westside / Verkhoshansky** — the
   methodology behind the strongest powerlifters of the last 40
   years, with its origins in Soviet sports science of the
   1960s–80s.

Both deserve a section in the [Exercise Library](/apps/exercise-library)
and a place in your weekly programming. Here's the tour.

## I — Tendon resilience: the school of Ben Patrick

### The premise

The standard strength curriculum trains muscles. **It barely
trains tendons.** Tendons remodel slowly (months, not weeks),
respond to specific stimuli, and are the limiting factor for
most lifters past 35.

If you've ever:
- Heard a pop in your patella squatting
- Felt a "twang" in your Achilles sprinting
- Had patellar tendinopathy ("jumper's knee")
- Had Achilles tendinopathy
- Had medial-knee pain when running

— you've met the consequences. Most lifters spend years building
quad mass without ever loading the patellar tendon through the
full range it needs to be resilient. Then a basketball pickup
game ends the lifting career.

> :sharpgoose: Muscle responds to load in weeks. Tendon responds
> in months. If you're not training tendons specifically, you
> have a strength imbalance that will eventually find you.

### Ben Patrick (Knees Over Toes Guy)

Ben Patrick's KOT method exploded in 2019 — viral basketball-prep
content. The principles:

1. **Train the knee through full range** including knee-over-toe.
   The whole "knees should never go past toes" advice from 1980s
   weight rooms is wrong; the tendon needs that range.
2. **Reverse sled drag**. Backward sled pulls are the
   purest-possible quad-tendon stimulus with **zero eccentric
   load** — perfect for rebuilding after injury or as a daily
   GPP tool.
3. **Tibialis raise**. The under-trained shin muscle. KOT made
   it mainstream; the result is fewer shin splints and better
   knee tracking.
4. **Slant-board work**. Heels on a 25° slant; deep ATG squats.
   Maximum VMO recruitment.
5. **Patrick step**. Slow eccentric step-downs from a deficit.
   The poster-child KOT exercise.

### Classical HSR — the research-validated companion

Long before KOT went viral, Per Kongsgaard and Stig Magnusson
(Copenhagen) ran the studies that established **heavy slow
resistance (HSR)** as the gold-standard tendon-rehab protocol:

- 3 seconds concentric, 3 seconds eccentric per rep
- 6 reps per set, 4 sets, building to ~85% 1RM
- 3 sessions per week, 12 weeks minimum

Tendons reorganize their collagen architecture under this exact
loading pattern. No bouncing, no plyometrics until the tendon
has remodeled.

The Alfredson protocol (eccentric calf raises for Achilles
tendinopathy) is the prototype — slow eccentrics, daily, for
12+ weeks.

> + one reverse sled drag = a 20-minute tendon session. Slot it
> in twice a week. Your knees stop hurting in 6-8 weeks.

### Programming

Tendon work is **prehab/maintenance, not the main lift**. The
typical placement:

- **Warm-up** (5-10 min): tibialis raise, ATG split squat
  bodyweight, Spanish squat.
- **Accessory work** (post-strength): 2-3 sets of Patrick step
  or slant-board squat.
- **Off-day GPP**: 20 min of reverse sled drag at moderate load.

Two or three sessions per week is plenty. Consistency over months
matters more than intensity in any single session.

## II — Soviet conjugate: the school of Verkhoshansky & Simmons

### The premise

Linear periodization (build to a 1RM peak, then reset) was the
Western default for decades. Soviet sports scientists working with
weightlifters and track athletes in the 60s–80s produced a better
system: **train multiple qualities simultaneously, on rotating
focuses, with accommodating resistance**.

Louie Simmons of Westside Barbell adapted it for powerlifting in
the 80s. Yuri Verkhoshansky published the canonical Soviet
methodology in *Supertraining* (with Mel Siff) and *Special
Strength Training Manual for Coaches*. Pavel Tsatsouline's RKC
imported the kettlebell side of the same tradition.

> :angrygoose: "Western" lifters were running 5×5 progressions
> and complaining about plateaus in 1985 while the Soviets had
> already published a complete system for working through them.
> The information was there — the translation lag was 30 years.

### The four-day Westside template

A canonical week:

| Day | Focus | Key concept |
|---|---|---|
| **Mon** | Max Effort lower-body | Work up to 1-3 RM on a rotated lift (box squat, deficit DL, banded GM) |
| **Wed** | Max Effort upper-body | 1-3 RM on a rotated press (floor press, pin press, board press) |
| **Fri** | Dynamic Effort lower-body | 8-12×2 speed squat with bands, 50-60% bar weight |
| **Sun** | Dynamic Effort upper-body | 8-9×3 speed bench, 50-60% bar weight + bands |

Plus accessory work after each session. The accessories don't
change weekly; the main lift rotates every 1-3 weeks to prevent
neural staleness.

### The Soviet/Westside canon

| Exercise | Concept | Soviet/Westside lineage |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed Bench (DE)** | Dynamic Effort | Compensatory acceleration on every rep |
| **Speed Squat (DE)** | Dynamic Effort | Box squat + bands at 50-60% |
| **Speed Deadlift (DE)** | Dynamic Effort | Pull from floor as fast as possible |
| **Banded Box Squat** | Accommodating resistance | Heavier at lockout via bands |
| **Banded Bench Press** | Accommodating resistance | Same, upper body |
| **Banded Good Morning** | Posterior chain ME | Westside lower-body accessory |
| **Reverse Hyper** | Spinal decompression + GHR | Louie Simmons' signature invention |
| **Belt Squat** | Heavy quad, no spinal load | Sorinex/Westside |
| **Box Squat** | Sit-back, accelerate up | Westside main-day staple |
| **Glute Ham Raise** | Posterior chain isolation | Soviet origin |
| **Depth Jump** | Reactive strength | Verkhoshansky's shock method |
| **Altitude Drop** | Reactive prep | Stick the landing, no bounce |
| **Cluster Set Squat** | Volume @ 85%+ | Soviet intensity-density |
| **Wave Loading (3-2-1)** | CNS potentiation | Two ascending waves |
| **Pendulum Snatch** | Reactive Olympic | Bondarchuk principle |
| **RKC Plank** | Tension training | Pavel Tsatsouline / RKC |
| **Long-Cycle Clean & Jerk (KB)** | Conditioning + strength | RKC competition standard |

### Core principles to internalize

**1. Compensatory acceleration.** Every rep is moved as fast as
possible regardless of load. A 90% squat moved with maximum
intent recruits the same fast-twitch fibers as a 50% squat moved
the same way. Speed-strength carries over to maximal strength.

**2. Accommodating resistance.** Bands and chains add load
through the range — heaviest where you're strongest (lockout),
lightest where you're weakest (bottom). Trains you to accelerate
through the full ROM.

**3. The conjugate rotation.** Main lift changes every 1-3 weeks
(floor press → board press → pin press → reverse-band bench →
back to floor press). Accessories stay constant. This dodges the
"frequency × intensity = staleness" trap of Western linear
programs.

**4. ME (Max Effort) ≠ rep maxes.** A 1RM attempt on box squat
isn't the same lift as a 1RM on free squat. Rotating ME variants
lets you go heavy weekly without burning out on the *same*
specific lift.

**5. Dynamic Effort is mandatory.** Most Western trainees skip
it. Westside lifters never skip it. Speed work isn't an option;
it's how you stay explosive.

> :surprisedgoose: Westside Barbell produced more 1000-lb squats
> in one gym than the entire rest of the USA combined for two
> decades. The methodology *works*. The American powerlifting
> establishment ignored it because Louie Simmons was abrasive
> and the system "didn't look like" traditional training.

### Programming

If you want to try the Soviet conjugate style without going full
Westside, the cheapest entry is:

- **Monday**: heavy day on your existing main lift (1×5 or 3×3)
- **Wednesday**: light/speed day — 6×3 at 60% with explosive intent
- **Friday**: accessory + reverse-hyper + GHR + tibialis

That's a poor-man's conjugate week and it works. The full system
needs bands, chains, a reverse-hyper machine, and a coach who
knows the program. Most commercial gyms don't have the equipment.

## III — Where these slot into the Goose Factor toolkit

The exercise library has **300+ exercises** now, including the
~22 just added for these two traditions. Search any of them by
name in the Exercise Library — every entry has the methodology
cited in its notes field.

In the [Routine Generator](/apps/routine-generator) and
[Session Builder](/apps/session-builder), tendon work tends to
land in the warm-up / pump phase; Soviet ME lifts land in the
heavy phase; DE lifts land in the heavy phase too but with the
speed bias noted.

If you're building a personal weekly template:

- **Two days of tendon work** as warm-up or off-day GPP. Reverse
  sled drag is the highest-leverage single exercise for knee
  durability.
- **One Westside-style ME day per lift** — rotate the variant
  every 2-3 weeks.
- **One DE day per lift** for speed-strength.

Most lifters who combine these report fewer joint issues, better
1RMs within 8-12 weeks, and meaningful resilience gains by month
3-4.
