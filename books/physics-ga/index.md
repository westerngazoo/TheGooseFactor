# Physics through Geometric Algebra

> A working physicist's reformulation of classical and quantum
> physics using geometric algebra — following the curriculum of
> Doran & Lasenby's *Geometric Algebra for Physicists* (Cambridge,
> 2003) but rewritten in the Goose Factor study-journal voice.

This book is not a competitor to Doran-Lasenby — that's the
canonical reference. It's a **reading companion**: the same
material with more inline intuition, fewer prerequisites, and a
compressed presentation that focuses on the headline results.

## Who this book is for

- **Physics students** who learned Maxwell's equations as four
  separate equations and got the strong feeling they should be one.
- **Engineers** who use quaternions for rotations and are
  curious about what's underneath.
- **Mathematicians** who find tensor calculus's index gymnastics
  uncomfortably arbitrary.
- **Working researchers** who suspect there's a cleaner framework
  for spinors but haven't had time to translate.

## What we cover

| Part | Topic | Headline result |
|---|---|---|
| **I** — Foundations refresh | Vectors, blades, geometric product | Two-axiom derivation |
| **II** — Classical mechanics | Newtonian dynamics, central forces, rigid bodies | Rotors as the orientation algebra |
| **III** — Spacetime algebra | STA basis, Lorentz boosts, 4-vectors | Boosts are hyperbolic rotors |
| **IV** — Electromagnetism | $F = E + IB$, Maxwell's equations | $\nabla F = J$ — one equation |
| **V** — Quantum theory | Pauli, Dirac, multiparticle | Spinors are even multivectors |
| **VI** — Gauge gravity | GTG (Lasenby-Doran-Gull) | GR in flat spacetime |
| **VII** — Geometric calculus | Vector derivative, manifolds, integration | Stokes & divergence in one statement |

Each part is independent enough that you can dip in. Part IV
(electromagnetism) is probably the most rewarding first read if
you have any background in classical EM.

## What we don't do

- We don't re-derive results from scratch. Where Doran-Lasenby
  has the proof, we cite chapter and section and summarize.
- We don't compete on rigor. The original is more rigorous; we
  trade some rigor for accessibility.
- We don't omit the math. This is still a physics book — the
  equations are the work.

## Prerequisites

- The basic GA framework. Read at least sections 1, 2, and 4 of
  the [Geometric Algebra study journal](/geometric-algebra) first,
  or watch [Mathoma's playlist](/geometric-algebra/video-companion).
- Undergraduate physics (Newtonian mechanics, intro EM, intro QM).
- Comfort with calculus, including some vector calculus.

You do **not** need:
- Tensor calculus.
- Differential forms.
- Lie algebras (we'll introduce what we need).

## How to read

Each chapter follows the same shape:

1. **The classical statement** — what physics says, in standard
   notation.
2. **The GA reformulation** — the same content, in GA notation.
3. **Why the reformulation is better** — what unifies, what
   simplifies, what new connections appear.
4. **Worked computation** — a concrete example end-to-end.

Doran-Lasenby cross-references appear inline as [D-L §X.Y].

> :weightliftinggoose: Physics in GA is the same physics. The
> shape changes, the answers don't. What you're paying for is a
> language where the next theorem is one line of algebra instead
> of three pages of index gymnastics.
