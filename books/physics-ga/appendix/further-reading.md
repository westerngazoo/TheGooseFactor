# Appendix D — Further Reading

Where to go after this book.

## The canonical references

### Doran & Lasenby, *Geometric Algebra for Physicists* (Cambridge, 2003)

The reference. Our book covers the same material with less depth
and more attitude; D-L is the place to go for full derivations,
exercises, and the precise sign conventions. The book is
expensive in print but worth it if you're serious about GA. PDF
preprints of selected chapters are available online.

### Hestenes, *Spacetime Algebra* (Reidel, 1966; updated by Birkhäuser, 2015)

The original. David Hestenes invented (or rediscovered) the
modern GA framework starting in the 1960s. *Spacetime Algebra*
is the seed work. Older notation (some signs differ from D-L)
but historically important.

### Hestenes & Sobczyk, *Clifford Algebra to Geometric Calculus* (Reidel, 1984)

The mathematical foundations. More abstract than D-L; covers
the linear-algebra, calculus, and topology underpinnings of GA.
Heavy on proofs; light on physics applications.

### Lounesto, *Clifford Algebras and Spinors* (Cambridge, 2nd ed. 2001)

The mathematician's reference. Discusses Clifford algebras
abstractly with extensive treatment of spinor representations.
Less physics-flavored than D-L but rigorous and complete.

## Modern textbooks and pedagogical resources

### Macdonald, *Linear and Geometric Algebra* (CreateSpace, 2010)

A gentle introduction at the undergraduate level. Bridges
classical linear algebra to GA. Good for first-year-undergrad to
math-prepared-non-physicist.

### Macdonald, *Vector and Geometric Calculus* (CreateSpace, 2012)

The calculus follow-up to the above. Covers vector derivative,
integration theorems, and basic differential geometry in GA
language.

### Dorst, Fontijne, & Mann, *Geometric Algebra for Computer Science* (Morgan Kaufmann, 2007)

The computer-graphics / robotics application book. Heavy on CGA
(conformal geometric algebra) for geometric primitives, screws,
and rigid-body kinematics. The reference for the practical
applications side of GA.

## Online resources

### bivector.net

Tutorials, interactive demos, and an active community. The
single best starting point for learning GA online.

### "An Introduction to Geometric Algebra" by Alan Bromborsky

A free PDF that's been recommended as a gentle intro for years.
Around 200 pages.

### sudgylacmoe (YouTube)

Animated video tutorials on GA. Particularly good for visualizing
the geometric product and rotor actions.

### "From Clifford Algebras to Spinors" (Lasenby's online lectures)

Cambridge undergraduate lectures, recorded and put online.
Lasenby's lectures are the canonical pedagogy.

## Application areas

### Physics

- Doran, Lasenby, Gull, et al. papers on **GTG** — gauge theory
  gravity. Searchable on arXiv.
- Hestenes, *New Foundations for Classical Mechanics* (1986). The
  classical-mechanics-in-GA book.
- *Clifford Algebras and Their Applications in Mathematical
  Physics* — conference proceedings, multi-volume.

### Computer graphics / robotics

- **gafro** library (C++) — robotic manipulation in CGA.
- **Ganja.js** (JavaScript) — interactive GA in the browser.
- **Gaalop** — GA → executable code compiler.

### Machine learning

- Brandstetter et al., *Clifford Neural Layers for PDE Modeling*
  (NeurIPS 2022).
- Ruhe et al., *Geometric Clifford Algebra Networks* (ICML 2023).
- See the **[AI through GA](/ai-ga)** book for ML-specific
  coverage.

### Quantum mechanics & QFT

- Hestenes, *Geometry of the Dirac Theory* — long-form treatment
  of Dirac theory in STA.
- Various Doran-Lasenby Cambridge group papers on GA and quantum
  field theory.

## Where GA is going

Active research areas where GA plays a central role:

1. **Quantum gravity** — GTG-style approaches to combining QFT
   with gravity.
2. **Computer graphics and animation** — CGA for skinning,
   skeletal animation, smooth pose interpolation.
3. **Machine learning** — Clifford and equivariant networks for
   geometric / molecular data.
4. **Robotics** — screw motor kinematics, motion planning in
   rotor spaces.
5. **Conformal field theory** — relations to twistor and conformal
   methods.

GA hasn't broken into mainstream physics pedagogy yet. Whether
it will is unclear; the inertia of "tensor index notation" is
considerable. But the conceptual elegance is hard to argue with
once you see it.

## Final word

The geometric-algebra framework is, in the end, an algebraic
**lens** on physics — not a different physics. Predictions match
tensor and matrix formulations exactly; the value-add is
conceptual clarity, computational compactness, and structural
uniformity across domains.

Whether that's worth the cost of learning a new notation depends
on you. If you're a working physicist who needs to compute, the
existing tools work fine. If you're trying to understand the
deep structure of geometry, GA might be your way in.

This book is the parallel translation. The original is your
choice.
