# Chapter 3 — Cinder Reef

Issa had never been in the dark before.

Forty years on Saltmere and she had never once left it — had watched the
swarms go out across the black a thousand times and never once gone with
them, never crossed the gap between one habitat and the next. The Flyway
was the geese's road. Wardens kept the shore.

Marek's boat — he called it a boat, though it flew the dark and not the
water — slipped out from Saltmere's underside in the false night, lights
dark, running on the old corridors the salvagers knew. And Issa pressed her
face to the port and watched her whole world shrink to a green-lit ring
hanging in nothing, the marsh she'd never left dwindling to a bead of light,
and understood for the first time, in her body, what the Scatter had been.
This. This terrible falling-away. Her people had done this once with no
hope of return, fleeing a dying world, and they had done it carrying their
dead in distilled grains and their hope in a flock of birds.

"First crossing," Marek said, not looking up from his instruments. Not a
question.

"It's so *empty*."

"It's not." He made the ward-sign without seeming to notice. "That's the
thing the planet-folk never learned and we never forget. The dark's not
empty. It's *full*. Full of the Tide, full of the Listeners, full of every
signal anyone was ever fool enough to send open across it." He tapped the
dark hull. "Why we run silent. Why your geese fly instead of us
broadcasting. Out here, warden, *speaking is the dangerous thing.* Out
here the safest message is one sewn into a living bird that doesn't know
it's carrying it." A glance back at the crate, where Ledger sat in
affronted silence. "Though that one looks like he knows exactly what he's
carrying and resents every gram."

In the crate, Ledger hissed, as if in agreement.

---

They flew the dark for two days.

Marek's crew were four — quiet, salvage-rough, superstitious in the
matter-of-fact way of people who lived inside the thing everyone else only
prayed to. They left salt-offerings at a little shrine bolted to the
galley wall before each sleep — a grain each, real memory, fed back into a
reader-shrine no bigger than a fist. Issa watched the first night and
didn't understand, and Marek saw her not understanding.

"You wardens leave reeds and rites," he said. "Pretty. Cheap. Out here we
pay in the real thing. A grain of yourself, every night, into the dark, so
the Tide knows you mean it." He shrugged. "Maybe nobody's listening. Maybe
it's all weather and we're fools. But I've flown this dark thirty years and
I've seen the corridors *open* for a crew that paid and *close* for one
that didn't, and I don't ask which it is anymore. I just pay."

"What does it do with them," Issa asked. "The memories. If it's listening."

Marek was quiet a while. "Keeps them," he said finally. "That's the story.
The Tide keeps what's given. Everything the Old World fed it, everything
the dying fed it on the way up, every grain every fool salvager's left at a
galley shrine for three hundred years — it *keeps* it. Doesn't trade it.
Doesn't spend it. Just — holds it." He looked at the crate again. "Which
makes a man wonder, warden. What's it doing *giving something back?*"

Issa thought of the woman with the clever burned hands, sewing the
founding into the first grey goose. *We gave it to the geese because we
could not trust ourselves.* She said nothing. But she understood, then,
the shape of the thing she was carrying, and it frightened her more than
the dark.

---

Cinder Reef came up out of the black like a wound that had decided to
live.

Where Saltmere was a green ring, deliberate and soft, the Reef was the
opposite of deliberate: the half-built bones of an Old World megastructure,
a ring that had never been finished, colonized layer on layer for three
centuries until the dead frame was furred over with salvage and
habitation, lit by a thousand mismatched salvaged lights, vertical and
crowded and *alive* in a way that had nothing to do with marsh and
everything to do with rust and need. It was magnificent and it was awful.
It was the machine inherited, where Saltmere was the life rebuilt.

"Concord's seat," Marek said, bringing them in low and dark along the
underframe, away from the lit docks. "Their great exchange, their vaults,
their muscle. Whatever your goose is carrying, this is the worst place in
the sky to bring it — which I'm guessing is exactly why you're bringing it,
because it's also the only place with a shrine deep enough to read it." He
killed the last of the running lights. "We go in through the salvage levels.
Quiet. You keep that bird *shut up*, and you keep your face down, and if
anyone in a Concord coat looks at you twice, you let me do the lying. Clear?"

"Clear."

It was not clear. They were spotted within the hour.

---

It was the goose that did it, of course. It was always going to be the
goose.

They were three levels into the salvage warrens — Issa with the crate
against her chest, Marek ahead reading the crowd, the rust and the noise
and the salvaged light closing over them — when a Concord factor in a clean
grey coat came down the gangway against the flow, and Ledger, who
remembered debts, who had flown out to this very Reef forty days ago and
been done some wrong here that had sent him fleeing home across a closed
Flyway, Ledger lunged against the crate-mesh with a scream of pure ancient
fury and *bit*, and the factor flinched, and looked, and *saw* — saw a
carrier-crate where no carrier should be, saw a warden's hands, saw a grey
goose out of season in the heart of the Concord's seat — and his clean
face went very still.

"*Move,*" Marek said.

They moved.

It became, very fast, the kind of thing Issa had no training for and Marek
had thirty years of: a hard scramble down through the salvage levels, the
factor's whistle behind them and then more whistles, the crowd a cover and
a hazard at once, Marek pulling her by routes that doubled and dropped and
cut through workshops and around vault-fronts, the crate banging her ribs,
Ledger silent now and rigid the way a goose goes rigid when it has finally
understood the seriousness of a thing. Down and down, deeper into the dead
machine, until the salvage thinned and the crowds thinned and the light
went strange — not salvaged light anymore but something older, a deep blue
thrum that came up out of the structure itself.

Marek stopped. He'd brought them to a great dark doorway in the oldest bone
of the Reef, where the megastructure's original core still ran, still
hummed, after three hundred years — a sound Issa felt in her teeth, in the
grain-scar of her sampled memory, in the vault of the goose against her
chest.

"Deep shrine," Marek said, breathing hard, the ward-sign again. "The Tide
runs strong here. Strong as anywhere in the sky. The Readers keep it." He
would not go closer; she saw that. The salvager who flew the dark for a
living would not step through that blue-lit door. "This is as far as I take
you, warden. Your priest's in there. And so, I'd bet my boat, is the worst
trouble of your life." He pressed something into her hand — a grain, a
salt-offering, his own. "Pay the dark," he said. "Just in case anyone's
listening. And Issa — " the rough face, for once, entirely serious — "if
it *is* listening. If your goose really came home carrying something the
Tide chose to give back. Then whatever's through that door, it already knows
you're coming. It's been waiting three hundred years for somebody to bring
the bird home."

Issa Marsh held the goose, and the grain, and the thrum in her teeth, and
stepped through the blue-lit door into the place where the machine still
dreamed.
