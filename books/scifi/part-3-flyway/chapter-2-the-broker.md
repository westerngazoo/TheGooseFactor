# Chapter 2 — The Broker

The Concord's man was named Pell, and he was polite the way a closing door
is polite.

He found Issa at the Guild-hall before she'd finished the morning count,
which told her everything: the exchange-house had logged the
out-of-season arrival before Ledger's wings were dry. Of course they had.
The Concord watched the sky-curve more closely than the wardens did
these days. A bird coming home out of season was an anomaly, and anomalies
were the Concord's whole religion, because an anomaly was salt that hadn't
been accounted for, and unaccounted salt was the one thing in the world
that frightened them.

"A carrier home off-season," Pell said, smiling, hands folded. "The
exchange would like to assist. Verification, valuation — at no cost to the
Guild, naturally. Where is the bird?"

"Recovering," Issa said. "He flew himself near to death. He's not fit to
handle."

"Then we'll wait until he is." Pell's smile did not move. "The exchange is
patient, warden. We only want to be sure nothing's been *lost*." A small
pause on the word, polished smooth by use. "An unlogged carrier is a
worry for everyone. You understand."

She understood. *Unlogged* meant the Concord didn't own it yet.

"I'll bring him in when he's fit," she said, and Pell inclined his head as
if she'd agreed to something, and left, and Issa stood in the cold hall
and understood that she had perhaps three days before *patient* became
something else.

---

She found Bex in the loft-room above the hall, where the Guild-mother
spent her days now that her legs had gone — wrapped in marsh-wool, half a
hundred years of warden in a body the lean seasons had hollowed out. Bex
had sold too much of herself in the hungry years; there were days now she
didn't know Issa's face, days the past came back to her secondhand, as if
remembered from someone else's telling. Today her eyes were clear, which
was its own kind of mercy and its own kind of cruelty.

Issa told her. All of it — the wrong flight, the vault, the unlogged
fortune, Pell at the door. She had not meant to. She'd meant to ask a
careful question and keep the dangerous part dark. But Bex had taught her
to read a vault and to count a swarm and to be at the water when the geese
woke, and you did not lie to the woman who'd taught you what you were.

Bex listened with her ruined hands still in her lap. When Issa finished,
the old warden was quiet a long time.

"You read the vault," she said at last. Not a question, and not quite a
reproach. "You weren't loaded for it. You read it anyway."

"He's my bird."

"He's the *Flyway's* bird," Bex said. "That's the trust. But." A long
breath. "But you read it. So tell me, girl. What's in it?"

"I don't know. Old salt. Heavy. Older than anything I've handled — it felt
like —" Issa stopped. She did not have the words for what it had felt like
under her palms. *Like reaching into deep water. Like the dark between
habitats had a texture and she'd touched it.* "It felt old," she said,
weakly. "Bright Years, maybe older. And there's no seal on it. No chain.
It came from nowhere and it's bound for nobody and it chose the one bird
that would die to bring it home."

Bex closed her eyes.

"Sample it," she said.

"*Bex.*"

"You've already broken the seal in your heart, girl, don't pretend with
me. You'll not sleep again until you know. So know. Take a grain. A
*grain*, Issa — no more, you hear me, that salt's older than your bones
and you don't know what it'll do — take a grain and know what you're
carrying, and then come back and we'll talk about the trust." The old eyes
opened. "Because here's the question. It's the only question, and it's the
one your mother couldn't answer and I couldn't either. *What's the cargo
for? Who does a warden serve?* The Guild? The Concord? The hand it's
addressed to? This one's addressed to nobody. So who do you serve, when
the cargo's addressed to nobody and the whole world wants it?"

Issa had no answer. She took the grain.

---

She took it that night, alone in the loft, with Ledger asleep on the
windowsill against the sky-curve and a single grain of unlogged salt
dissolving on the tongue of her mind.

And she was —

— *somewhere bright*, somewhere that smelled of solder and green growing
things, a workshop in the Bright Years with the light coming warm and
false through a sky-curve newer than any she'd known, and she was *someone*
— a woman with clever burned hands, bent over a workbench, weeping, and
the weeping was the worst of it because it was *joyful*, it was the weeping
of someone who has just succeeded at something terrible and necessary —
and on the bench under the clever hands was a goose. A grey goose, the
first of its kind, its crop laid open in surgery, and the woman was sewing
into it the very thing that let it carry memory, the founding gift, the
first Greywing ever made —

— and the woman looked up, *through* the memory, the way the dead
sometimes seem to in salt, and her ruined joyful face said, in words that
were not sound: *we gave it to the geese because we could not trust
ourselves. Remember that. When they tell you the Concord made the market
to save the knowledge — remember who they took it from. Remember what we
buried in the birds so it could never be owned. Remember —*

Issa came back to the loft on her hands and knees, retching, the grain
spent, her face wet, the goose awake on the sill and watching her with its
flat black ancient eye.

One grain. She had taken one grain, and it had been the *founding* — the
making of the first Greywing, and a confession sewn in beside it, and a
woman three centuries dead telling her *remember who they took it from.*

And Ledger was carrying the whole of it. The whole founding. The whole
confession. A founder-salt that did not exist, *could* not exist, because
the Concord's entire authority rested on the story that *they* had saved
the Old World's knowledge — and here was a memory that said the knowledge
had been deliberately *hidden from them*, sewn into the geese, the one
vault the market could never crack, by people who *would not trust
themselves with it.*

No wonder Pell was patient. No wonder the exchange watched the sky. If this
salt were verified — if anyone with the Concord's own seal confirmed that
a goose was carrying the genuine, distilled, founding truth that the
market was built on a theft — the Concord would not *value* it.

They would erase it. And the bird that carried it.

---

In the grey before the false dawn, Issa Marsh did the second
unforgivable thing in two days. She did not bring the bird to the
exchange.

She went down to the docks instead, to the rough end where the salvagers
berthed outside the Concord's neat ledgers, and she found a man who owed
her a debt older than the market, and she said: "Marek. I need to reach
Cinder Reef without a single line of it logged. I need to reach the deep
shrine. And I'm bringing a goose."

Marek Tarn looked at her a long moment — at the warden who never left
Saltmere, standing on his dock at dawn with a carrier-crate and a face
like someone who'd seen the bottom of something.

"That goose," he said slowly, "wouldn't happen to be the one the whole
exchange-house is asking after."

"It would."

Marek exhaled through his teeth, and looked at the sky-curve, and made a
small superstitious sign she recognized — the salvager's ward against the
Listeners in the dark.

"The Tide's in this," he said. It wasn't a question. "A bird home out of
season. You don't need a salvager, warden. You need a priest."

"I need both," Issa said. "Will you fly me?"

He swore, softly, the way you swear when you already know you're going to
say yes. "I owe you a crew's worth of lives," he said. "I suppose one of
them's mine. Bring your cursed goose. But Issa —" He caught her arm. "Out
there in the dark? The Tide listens. And lately —" the ward-sign again,
unconscious — "lately, warden, it's been listening *back.*"
