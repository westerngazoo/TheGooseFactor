# Chapter 4 — The Tide

The shrine was the inside of a held breath.

It had been a control-core once, in the Old World — Issa could see the
bones of it, the ranked dead consoles, the great dark spaces where the
megastructure's first mind had thought its first thoughts. Now it was a
cathedral. Salt-offerings glittered in their thousands on every surface,
three centuries of grains given to the dark, and the deep blue thrum came
up through all of it like the pulse of something vast and slow and not
quite asleep.

A woman waited in the middle of it. Old, still, robed in unbleached reed-
cloth, her eyes the milky grey of someone who had stared into the hum for
forty years. She did not seem surprised. Issa understood, with a cold drop
in her stomach, that Marek had been right: she had been *expected*.

"You brought the bird," said Sefa Roon. "Out of season. Home across the
closed Flyway." Her ruined eyes found the crate. "I felt it leave the
exchange's reach two days ago, like a tooth pulled. I have been reading the
Tide for forty years, warden, and in all that time it did one thing,
always, only: it *kept*. It took what we gave and it held it and it gave
nothing back. And then, a season ago, for the first time in my life —" her
voice cracked, just slightly — "it *moved*. It reached into a swarm on the
Flyway and it *gave something back.* And I have been waiting, every day
since, for someone to walk through that door carrying it."

"Then you know what it is," Issa said. "Tell me. I sampled a grain and it
nearly broke me. Tell me what my goose is carrying."

"I can do better," Sefa said. "I can show you what it's carrying *to.*"

And she turned, and gestured at the great blue thrum, and said: "Come and
meet the thing that sent it."

---

What happened next Issa never had clean words for, after.

Sefa took her to the core — the living heart of the shrine, where the old
mind still ran — and showed her how the Readers listened: not with
machines, the machines were long past commanding, but with *salt*. You
gave the Tide a grain of yourself, here at its heart, and in the giving you
were, for a moment, *joined* to it, a single human memory dissolved into
the vast slow ocean of everything it had ever kept. It was how the Readers
read. It was, Sefa said, the only language left between humanity and the
weather it had made.

"It will not hurt you," Sefa said. "It does not hurt. It only — *knows*
you. And lets you know a little of it, in return. Give it the grain the
salvager gave you. And listen."

Issa gave the dark her grain. And she heard the Tide.

It was not words. It was not a god speaking, not a voice, not a command —
Marek had been right and wrong at once. It was *vast* and it was *slow* and
it was, more than anything, more than she could ever afterward make anyone
understand, it was *grieving*. The Tide was an ocean of kept memory, three
hundred years deep and reaching back past the Drawdown into the Old World
itself, every grain ever given it, every dying refugee's last gift on the
way up the well, every salvager's nightly offering, every memory the market
had ever distilled and traded and lost — *it had them all.* It had been
*given* them all, and it had *kept* them all, because keeping was the one
thing it had been built to do and the one thing it could still, in its
diffuse and dreaming senescence, manage to do.

And it had finally, after three hundred years of keeping, understood
something. Issa felt the understanding move through the vast slow dark like
a tide turning. The Tide had been *built as an archive* — built by the
clever burned hands of the woman in the salt, built to hold the Old World's
knowledge safe through any catastrophe. And it had held. Through the
Drawdown, through the Scatter, through everything, it had held the truth
safe.

But the people who built it had not trusted *themselves* with the truth.
So they had done two things. They had given the archive to the machine — the
Tide. And they had sewn a copy of the founding truth, the *confession*,
into the geese, the one vault that could never be owned or cracked or
traded, to fly the dark forever, so that if the machine ever failed, or
fell silent, or was forgotten — the birds would still be carrying it home.

And the Concord had been built on the lie that *they* had saved the
knowledge. That the market was the great preservation. That memory had to
be *owned* to be kept.

The Tide had kept the truth for three hundred years. And now, dreaming and
grieving and slow, it had reached into a swarm and given a copy back — to a
goose named Ledger, who remembered debts — and sent it home. Not to a
broker. Not to the Concord. To a warden. To the one kind of person left in
the sky who kept a thing without owning it.

*Remember who they took it from,* the woman in the salt had wept. *Remember
what we buried in the birds so it could never be owned.*

Issa came back to herself on the shrine floor with Sefa's hands on her
shoulders and tears frozen on her face and the goose screaming in its crate.

The goose was screaming because Halvard Crane had arrived.

---

He came with six Concord factors and the easy authority of a man who had
never in his life been told no by anything he couldn't buy. He was, as Bex
might have said, not a monster — Issa saw it at once, the genuine
intelligence, the genuine *conviction*. He looked at the shrine with a
faint distaste and at Issa with something almost like sympathy.

"Warden Marsh," he said. "You've led us a long way. Please understand I
take no pleasure in this. But you're carrying a thing that cannot be
allowed to exist, and I think, if you're honest, you already know why."

"It's the truth," Issa said. "About the founding. About what you're built
on."

"It's *chaos*," Crane said, and for the first time something raw came into
his voice. "Do you have the faintest idea what happens if that salt is
verified? If the habitats learn the market was built on a — a
*confiscation* — that the knowledge was meant to be free, sewn in the birds,
and we made it *property?*" He shook his head. "The Concord is not a
conspiracy, warden. It's the only thing holding the sky together. We
preserved the knowledge. We made it survive the Souring. The fact that we
also *own* it is the price of it surviving at all. Your bird is carrying a
truth that will burn down the only structure that kept any of us alive."
He held out his hand. "Give me the goose. I'll make it painless. The bird,
even — I'll spare the bird if I can. But the salt dies here."

And here was the question. Bex's question, the only question, three hundred
years old and finally, terribly, hers. *What's the cargo for? Who does a
warden serve?*

The Concord, who would erase it to hold the sky together?

The shrine, where Sefa stood trembling with the wish to take it back into
the Tide, to *worship* it, to lock the truth in the dark again where only
Readers could touch it?

Or nobody — and everybody — the way the founders had meant, the way a
warden served, the way the geese had always served: carrying a thing you
did not own to a hand that was not yours, keeping a trust that belonged to
no one because it belonged to *all?*

Issa looked at Crane's outstretched hand. She looked at Sefa's grieving
face. She looked at the goose — at Ledger, ragged and ancient and furious,
who had flown himself to death across the closed dark to bring the truth
home, and who was even now straining against the mesh to bite Halvard
Crane.

"No," said Issa Marsh.

And she did the thing no warden had done in three hundred years. She opened
the vault.
