# Chapter 1 — Out of Season

The false dawn came to Saltmere the way it always did: not as light but as
a slow warming of the sky-curve overhead, the habitat's managed morning
rising over the marsh like breath on glass. Issa Marsh was already at the
water.

She was always at the water. Forty years on the ring and she had never
once slept past the swarm's waking, never once let the geese rise to an
empty bank. Her mother had taught her that, and her mother's mother: *you
are there when they wake, or you are not their warden.* So she stood in
the reeds with the cold coming up through her boots, a bucket of feed-mash
souring in her hand, and watched the Saltmere swarm lift off the black
water in a clamor of grey and complaint.

Three hundred and twelve birds. She counted them the way she counted them
every morning, not on purpose, just the way other people breathed. The
swarm wheeled once over the marsh, loud as a market, and settled again to
feed. Three hundred and twelve. All present. All home.

The Flyway season was closed. Had been closed for forty days. The autumn
swarm — Bex's birds, the long-haul carriers — had gone out to Cinder Reef
and the far habitats six weeks back and would not return until the cold
turned. Issa's swarm were home-birds this season, marsh-keepers, and they
would not fly the dark again until spring. There was nothing on the
Flyway. There was nothing coming. The season was *closed*.

So when the three hundred and thirteenth bird came down out of the
sky-curve, Issa dropped the bucket.

---

It came wrong. That was the first thing she understood, before she
understood anything — it came *wrong*, low and hard and alone, no swarm
around it, no formation, just one bird falling out of the false morning
like something thrown. The home-birds scattered off the water screaming.
Issa was already running, mash forgotten, boots sucking in the marsh-mud,
because a goose flying like that was a goose dying, and she knew that
particular ugly desperate flight the way you know a sound from a
nightmare.

It hit the water badly, skidded, came to rest in the reeds twenty meters
out, and lay there with its wings half-open, heaving.

She went in after it without thinking. The water closed cold around her
waist. She talked the whole way — low, steady, the warden's nonsense
murmur that meant nothing and meant *I am here, I am here, you are home* —
and when she got her hands on the bird it tried, even then, ragged and
half-drowned, to bite her.

That was when she knew.

"Ledger," she said. "Oh, you stupid old bastard. *Ledger.*"

---

He should not have been there. That was the thing she kept circling, all
the way back to the bank with sixteen pounds of furious soaked gander
clutched to her chest, all through the careful hour in the warming-shed
with the lamps and the towels and the slow restoration of an animal that
had flown itself nearly to death. He *should not have been there.*

Ledger was a long-haul bird. He'd gone out with the autumn swarm, six
weeks back, bound for Cinder Reef and the far habitats — Bex's birds, not
hers, but Issa had raised him from a gosling before he'd graded into the
carriers, and he still came to her hand and still, after all these years,
still bit everyone who'd ever crossed him. Warden humor had named him: a
gander that remembered debts. He'd flown out forty-some days ago on a
closed loop that would not bring him home until the cold.

And here he was. Home. Alone. Out of season. Forty days early and a
thousand kilometers of dark too soon.

A swarm came home out of season the way a clock ran backward — it didn't,
it *couldn't*, and if it did, something was deeply and structurally
*wrong*. A single bird home out of season, alone, flown to ruin — Issa
had heard the old wardens speak of it, the way they spoke of all omens, in
the careful flattened voice you used for things you didn't want to call
down by naming. *A bird out of season is a wound in the rhythm.* Something
on the Flyway had gone wrong enough to break a carrier loose and send it
running home alone across the listening dark.

She got him dry. She got him warm. She got broth into him, and watched the
black panic go slowly out of his eyes and the ordinary outrage come back
in, and only then — only when he was Ledger again, hating her with his
whole heart and leaning into her hand — did she check the vault.

---

Every carrier had a crop-vault: the sealed biological strongroom where the
salt rode, the memory dissolved into inert carriage-form, keyed to a
recipient, unreadable without the living bird and the right key. You
didn't check a vault you hadn't loaded. It wasn't yours; it wasn't your
business; the whole sacred point of the geese was that what they carried
stayed dark until it reached the hand it was meant for. A warden who
cracked a vault she hadn't loaded was a warden no more.

But Ledger was *her* bird, gosling-raised, and he'd come home a thousand
clicks early and half-dead, and a wound in the rhythm was a wound, and Issa
Marsh had never in her life been able to leave a hurt thing un-looked-at.

She read the vault the way Bex had taught her, palms and patience, the old
slow sense of it. And she went cold all the way down.

Because the vault was *full*. Ledger was carrying salt — heavy salt, dense
salt, more than a single carrier should hold — and none of it was logged.
There was no manifest. No recipient-key she recognized. No Concord seal, no
Guild mark, no broker's chain. A carrier flew loaded *or* empty and
every gram was logged six ways before it left the ground, because salt was
money and money was tracked, always, by everyone.

Ledger was carrying a fortune in unlogged memory that no living person had
given him, home across a closed Flyway, forty days out of season, alone.

"Where did you *go*," Issa whispered. The gander looked at her with one
flat black eye and said nothing, the way geese say nothing, which is to
say enormously.

She should report it. That was the rule, clean and bright: unlogged salt
on a Guild bird went straight to the Guild-hall and from there, by treaty,
to the Concord's exchange, to be verified, valued, and entered. That was
the rule. That was the trust. That was forty years of it, and her mother's,
and her mother's mother's.

Issa sat in the warming-shed with the lamps low and her dead clothes
dripping and a grumpy ancient goose leaning warm against her ribs, and she
thought about the rule, and she thought about the wound in the rhythm, and
she thought — she didn't know why she thought it, except that her hands
had read something in the vault that her mouth couldn't yet say — she
thought: *if I report this, they will take him, and they will open the
vault to value it, and they will not let me keep the bird.*

And under that, quieter, in the place where she kept the heresies she'd
never said aloud:

*Somebody sent this home on purpose. Somebody chose the one bird in the
sky that would fly itself to death to reach me.*

She did not report it. She banked the lamps, and she carried Ledger up to
her own loft where the home-birds couldn't trouble him, and she sat awake
the whole of the false night with the goose asleep against her and the
unlogged fortune sealed in his living body, and she watched the sky-curve
and waited for the morning she already knew was going to be the last
ordinary one she'd have for a long, long time.

In the morning, the Concord's man came to ask about a goose.
