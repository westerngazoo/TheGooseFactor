# Chapter 5 — Migration

To open a vault is not to spill it. Issa knew that the way she knew the
morning count. A vault opened wrong denatured, the salt dying with the
seal. To open a vault *right* — to move its cargo, living, into another
carriage — that was warden's work, the oldest work, the work her hands
had done ten thousand times.

She had no other carrier. She had one goose and a shrine full of three
hundred years of given salt and a vast slow grieving ocean of a mind
thrumming up through the floor.

So she gave the truth to the Tide.

Not to *keep* — the Tide had kept it for three centuries and keeping was
the trap, keeping was the lie, keeping was just owning by another name. She
gave it to the Tide to *carry*. She knelt at the core with the goose's vault
open under her hands and Crane shouting and the factors moving and Sefa
crying out, and she did the only thing a warden knew how to do with a cargo
addressed to nobody: she *released it to the swarm.*

Because the Tide *was* a swarm. She understood that now, kneeling in the
blue thrum — the ocean of kept memory was nothing but the largest swarm in
the sky, every grain a bird, every offering a carrier, three hundred years
of distributed trust, and it had been waiting, *waiting*, for someone to
stop asking it to keep and start asking it to *fly.*

She poured the founding truth into the Tide and she gave it, with the last
of her own grain, the only instruction a warden ever gave a swarm:

*Carry this home. To every habitat. To every hand. Out of season. All of
them. Now. Go.*

And the Tide — the grieving, dreaming, three-hundred-year-silent Tide —
*moved.*

---

Across the sky, in that hour, every swarm rose.

It was the thing the histories would argue about for a generation after,
the thing the Readers would build a hundred conflicting doctrines on, the
thing Marek Tarn would tell in salvage-halls until he died and never once
be believed: that on a single night, out of season, in defiance of every
rhythm of the Flyway, *every Greywing swarm in the sky lifted at once* — and
flew. Not the routes. Not the seasons. They flew to every habitat, every
ring and reef and hab in the scattered dark, and they came down out of the
sky-curves into ten thousand astonished marshes, and in each one a single
bird came to a single hand carrying a vault that opened to the same grain:

the woman with the clever burned hands, weeping with terrible joy, sewing
the founding into the first grey goose, saying *we gave it to the geese
because we could not trust ourselves — remember who they took it from —
remember what we buried in the birds so it could never be owned.*

The truth went everywhere at once. To every hand. Owned by no one, because
it was carried by everyone. The way the founders meant. The way a warden
serves.

You cannot erase what the whole sky is carrying. You cannot buy back a
thing that has come home, out of season, to ten thousand marshes in a
single night. By the time Halvard Crane understood what she had done, it
was done, in every habitat in the sky, beyond any vault or seal or coat to
call it back.

He did not even seem angry, at the end. He seemed tired. "You've burned it
down," he said. "The only thing holding the sky together."

"No," said Issa. "I let it go home. That's not the same thing. You only
think it is because you've never kept anything you didn't own."

---

It cost her, of course. The world does not crack open for free.

It cost her Saltmere — she could not go back, not as warden, not with the
Concord's whole ruin laid at her feet; the Guild could not protect her and
would not have to, because by the time the factors thought to take her, she
was gone into the dark with Marek, who owed her a crew's worth of lives and
reckoned, in the end, that this had been most of them.

It cost her Bex — though that was the cold turning, not the Concord, the old
warden going out gentle in the loft-room on Saltmere with the last of her
borrowed memory spent, never knowing, or perhaps knowing perfectly, what
her girl had done. *What's the cargo for, girl? Who does a warden serve?*
Issa had her answer now. She'd carried it to every hand in the sky. She
hoped, wherever the old woman's thinning memory had gone, that some grain
of it had heard.

It did not cost her Ledger.

That was the mercy she had not earned and got anyway. The ancient gander
came through it all — the shrine, the rising, the long flight after into
the salvager's dark — ragged and furious and entirely, cussedly alive, and
he rode on Issa's shoulder out of the only world she'd ever known and bit
every single person who came too near, including, twice, Issa herself.

---

They settled, after — Issa and Marek and the crew and the goose — on no
habitat at all, but in the dark between them, on the salvager's boat that
flew the corridors and paid the Tide its nightly grain. Issa Marsh, who had
never left Saltmere in forty years, became a creature of the whole sky, of
the Flyway itself, of the long road between the scattered homes of a
scattered people.

The Concord did not fall, not all at once; systems that large fall slowly,
the way the Old World fell, by drawdown and not by bang. But the truth was
loose now, in every hand, and a market built on the lie that memory must be
owned to be kept had taken a wound it would not heal from. Other wardens
began, quietly, to ask Bex's question. Other swarms began, quietly, to fly
strange routes. And the Tide — the great grieving ocean of everything
humanity had ever given the dark — having once, after three hundred years,
remembered how to *give*, did not seem inclined to stop.

Sometimes, on the long flights, Issa would give the dark her nightly grain
the way Marek had taught her, and kneel a moment in the hum, and feel the
vast slow swarm of it turn around her. She never knew, truly, how much of
it was a mind and how much was weather. She had stopped needing to know.
It kept what it was given. And now, sometimes, it carried things home.

On the worst nights, the homesick ones, she would put her hand on the old
goose where he slept against the hull, and feel the warm absurd life of him,
this engineered descendant of a creature that had crossed dying worlds on
instinct since before there were people to engineer it, and she would think
about the founders' choice — to trust the truth not to a market, not even
to a god, but to a flock of cranky, faithful, unhackable birds who would
always, always, no matter the season, find their way home.

It was, she thought, the best decision anyone in the long sad brilliant
history of her species had ever made.

Ledger, sensing sentiment, bit her.

She laughed in the dark, and the swarm of the Tide turned slow around the
little boat, and somewhere out across the scattered sky the geese were
flying — out of season, off the routes, carrying the truth home to every
hand that would take it — and Issa Marsh, warden of no habitat and of all
of them, kept the night-watch on the Flyway, and was, against everything,
content.
