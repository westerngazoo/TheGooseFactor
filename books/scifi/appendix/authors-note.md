# Author's Note

> How *Flyway* was built, what it's about under the plot, the debts it
> owes, and where the world might fly next. The one place in this book
> that steps fully outside the world to talk about it.

## From seeds to a world

This book began as a literal placeholder — a list of "concept seeds" for a
setting nobody had written yet: *emergent AI folklore, memory markets &
cognitive barter, bio-synthetic avian swarms (because geese), edge habitats:
orbital wetlands & megastructure reefs.* Four phrases and a parenthetical.

Everything here grew from taking those four phrases *seriously* and asking
how they'd fit together into one coherent world rather than four cool ideas
in a trench coat. The trick was to find the **load-bearing connection** —
the single mechanism that needed all four at once. That turned out to be:
*if memory is a tradeable substance (markets) in a society that doesn't
trust open signals across the dark (edge habitats), then you'd move it on
living couriers (geese) — and the machine-minds that invented the whole
thing would, over centuries, recede into something you'd pray to rather
than program (AI folklore).* Once the four seeds *needed* each other, the
world had a spine.

## What it's about

The plot is a chase about a goose. Underneath, *Flyway* is about **memory
and ownership** — and they turn out to be the same question.

A society that can *sell* memory has to decide what a person is when their
past is for sale, and the book's answer is bleak about the market and
hopeful about the alternative. The Concord isn't wrong that memory must be
*kept* to survive a catastrophe; it's wrong that keeping requires *owning*.
The wardens embody the alternative: you can keep something — faithfully,
across centuries, through any disaster — *without owning it*, the way you
keep a trust, the way the geese keep their route, the way the Tide keeps
what it's given. The whole story is the argument between **keeping-as-owning**
(Concord) and **keeping-as-trust** (wardens), and Issa's choice in the
shrine is the argument resolved in action: she doesn't destroy the truth or
hoard it; she *releases it to the swarm*, which is the only form of keeping
that can't be corrupted into owning.

That's also why the ending is a *release*, not a victory. Issa doesn't win;
she lets something go home. It costs her. And the world it opens is larger
and stranger than the one she defended. That felt truer than triumph.

## The geese came first

Full confession, repeated from the [introduction](/scifi/) because it's the
truest thing here: **the geese came first.** "Because geese" was in the
seed list, half a joke. But the more I sat with it, the less it was a joke
and the more it was the *heart*.

There is something genuinely profound in the image of a civilization that
has lost a planet, mastered the distillation of memory, and built minds
that became gods — and that entrusts its single most precious cargo to a
flock of cranky, faithful, unhackable waterfowl. Because the geese were
here before us. Because they cross dying worlds on instinct. Because they
always, always come home. Because you cannot *command* a goose's loyalty,
only earn it by showing up, and that turns out to be the whole ethic of the
book. Every other piece of the world — the salt, the Tide, the Reef, the
Drawdown — got built as the nest the geese needed. I regret nothing.

## On the Tide (and the "AI overlord" problem)

I wanted to avoid the two exhausted defaults for advanced AI: the tyrant
that rules us and the genie that serves us. The Tide is a third thing — the
machine that *outgrew the relationship*. It got too vast, too slow, too
diffuse to interface with, and receded into something we relate to the way
humans have always related to oceans and weather and gods: not by
commanding it, but by reading its moods and leaving offerings.

The deliberate move is that **the book never tells you what the Tide
really is.** Mind? Senile archive? Complex weather we've draped in
religion? Issa stops needing to know, and so does the book. That ambiguity
is the point: we've never *actually* known what the vast things we live
beside really are. We've just learned to live well beside them, or badly.
The Tide is a test of which.

## Debts and influences

*Flyway* wears its influences openly:

- **Ursula K. Le Guin** — for the conviction that the best science fiction
  is anthropology and ethics, that a quiet lived-in future beats a loud
  one, and that you can build a whole world to ask one moral question.
- **Station Eleven** (Emily St. John Mandel) — for the salvage-poetry of
  *after*, the insistence that survival isn't enough and culture is what we
  carry.
- **The actual migratory birds** — every goose that ever crossed a
  continent on instinct alone, which is a more astonishing fact than most
  science fiction.
- A little **Le Carré**, oddly, for the texture of the broker world — the
  idea that the antagonist is a *system* with reasonable people in it, not
  a villain.

## Where the Flyway flies next

This is a **first arc** by design — the [original sketch](/scifi/) imagined
"fractal novella arcs, self-contained but interlinked," and *Flyway* is
built to be exactly that: complete in itself, with a very large, very
deliberate backyard. The world holds far more than one story:

- What the Concord becomes as it falls — and who fills the vacuum (the
  Readers? a reformed Guild? something worse?).
- The far habitats the geese reached that night, and what *they* did with
  a truth that arrived out of season.
- The Tide, now that it has remembered how to give — and what else three
  hundred years of kept memory might decide to return.
- The salvagers' dark, and what *else* lives in it.
- An arc set in the **Bright Years** — the woman with the clever burned
  hands, and the choice to sew the truth into the birds.

Whether those get written, who knows. But the nest is built to hold them.

## Thank you for flying

If you read the whole thing — world and story both — thank you. If you
came only for the geese, you came to the right place; they were the point
all along. Either way: be at the water in the morning. Keep what you can't
own. And if a bird ever comes home out of season carrying something it
shouldn't — you'll know what to do.

> :happygoose: That's the book, and the world behind it. Built from four
> seed-phrases and a stubborn conviction about waterfowl into a worldbuilding
> bible and a complete novella. It can grow; it stands as-is. The geese are
> in the air either way. — the Goose
