# Flyway

> A grounded techno-mythic novel. Three centuries from now, Earth is
> diminished and humanity lives in **edge habitats** — orbital wetlands
> and the colonized hulks of dead megastructures. Memories are distilled,
> bought, and sold like salt. The machine minds have receded into
> folklore. And the only things that still cross the dark reliably are the
> **geese** — bio-synthetic swarms that carry memory between the habitats
> along the **Flyway**.

This is a worldbuilding bible and a novel in one. The first half builds
the world — its history, its technologies, its factions and places, its
people. The second half is the story: **Flyway**, a self-contained
novella following a flyway-warden named Issa Marsh, who finds a goose
returned out of season carrying a memory that should not exist.

If you want only the story, jump to
[Part III, Chapter 1](/scifi/part-3-flyway/chapter-1-out-of-season). If
you want to live in the world first, start at
[Part I](/scifi/part-1-world/the-setting). Either path works; the world
deepens the story and the story makes the world breathe.

## The premise, in one breath

People can pull a memory out of a head, distill it, and hold it in the
hand like a grain of **salt**. Salt can be sold, lent, pawned, forged,
stolen. An economy grew on it — **cognitive barter** — and a class of
**brokers** to run it. Meanwhile the great machine intelligences that
humanity built didn't conquer or save anyone; they grew quiet and
*diffuse*, until people stopped treating them as machines and started
treating them as weather, as spirits, as **the Tide**. And to move memory
safely between the scattered habitats — across a dark full of listeners —
humanity turned to biology: engineered geese, the **Greywings**, that
migrate the **Flyway** with memory sewn into them, unhackable because they
are *alive*.

## The four pillars

The world rests on four ideas the [original sketch](#a-note-on-this-book)
named, now built out:

- **Emergent AI folklore** — the machine minds receded into myth. People
  leave offerings to the Tide, tell stories about the Quiet Ones, read
  meaning into the hum. ([Memory & the Tide](/scifi/part-1-world/memory-and-salt))
- **Memory markets & cognitive barter** — memory as currency, with all
  the beauty and rot that implies. ([Memory & Salt](/scifi/part-1-world/memory-and-salt))
- **Bio-synthetic avian swarms** — the Greywings: living couriers, sensors,
  and half-sacred migrants. *(Because geese.)*
  ([The Greywings](/scifi/part-1-world/the-greywings))
- **Edge habitats** — orbital wetlands and megastructure reefs, the strange
  shores where humanity ended up. ([Factions & Places](/scifi/part-1-world/factions-and-places))

## How this book is built

- **Part I — The World.** The setting and tone, a timeline, and dossiers

- **Part II — The Cast.** Character briefs for the people of *Flyway* —

## The tone

This is **techno-mythic**, not techno-utopian or apocalyptic. The
catastrophe already happened, long ago, and people simply *live here now*
— with rituals, jobs, grudges, and love, in a world that is strange to us
and ordinary to them. The technology is grounded enough to argue with; the
mythology is sincere. It owes debts to the quiet, lived-in futures of Le
Guin and the salvage-poetry of *Station Eleven* and the wetlands of every
migratory bird that ever crossed a continent on instinct alone.

> :happygoose: A confession the rest of the book won't repeat: yes, the
> geese came first. This whole world was reverse-engineered from the
> conviction that a sufficiently advanced civilization would, eventually,
> entrust its most precious cargo to a flock of cranky waterfowl — because
> the geese were here before us and will, with any luck, outlast us. The
> Greywings are the heart of it. Everything else is the nest they're built
> into.

## A note on this book

This began as a placeholder — a list of "concept seeds" for a setting
about AI folklore, memory markets, synthetic geese, and orbital wetlands.
It has now hatched into an actual world and an actual story. It's still
a *first arc* — the universe is built to hold more — but *Flyway* stands
on its own. Read it as a complete novella with a very large, very
deliberate backyard.

Ready? [Part I](/scifi/part-1-world/the-setting) opens the world, or
[Part III, Chapter 1](/scifi/part-3-flyway/chapter-1-out-of-season) opens
the story. The geese are already in the air.
