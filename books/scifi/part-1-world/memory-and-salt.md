# Memory & Salt

> Two of the world's strangenesses, deeply entwined: memory can be pulled
> from a mind and traded like **salt**, and the machine-minds that made
> the technology have receded into a diffuse presence people call **the
> Tide**. The first built an economy; the second became a religion. This
> dossier explains both, because in *Flyway* you cannot understand one
> without the other.

## 1. What memory distillation is

In the Bright Years ([Timeline](/scifi/part-1-world/timeline)), refugees
desperate to save what the Drawdown was erasing perfected **memory
distillation**: a process that reads a specific memory from a living mind
and renders it into a stable, portable substance — a fine, faintly
luminous crystalline grain. The street name stuck: **salt**.

A grain of salt is not a recording or a video. It is the *experience
itself*, distilled — when you take in salt (dissolve it, the rite goes,
"on the tongue of the mind"), you **live the memory** as if it were your
own: the smell of a lost garden, a stranger's first kiss, the schematic a
dead engineer never wrote down. Salt carries sensation, knowledge, and
feeling together, indivisibly. That indivisibility is its beauty and its
horror.

## 2. Distillation costs the original

The crucial, cruel fact: distilling a memory **degrades the original**.
Pull a memory to salt and the mind it came from is left with a *thinning*
— the event grows vague, secondhand, eventually gone, as if remembered
from someone else's telling. Strong distillation can take the memory
*entirely*.

So to sell a memory is, in a real sense, to *lose* it. People sell their
childhoods to pay debts and are left unable to recall their mothers'
faces. Some sell deliberately — to forget grief, to shed trauma (the
"mercy trade"). Some are *robbed* of memory, left with holes. This is the
moral engine of the whole setting: **memory is the one thing you spend by
keeping it elsewhere.**

> :surprisedgoose: This is the twist that makes the memory market more
> than a gimmick: it's not *copying*, it's *moving*. Distillation doesn't
> photograph a memory; it *extracts* it, leaving the donor diminished. So
> every transaction is a small amputation. A society that sells memory is
> a society where the poor are *literally less themselves* — they've sold
> their pasts to eat — and the rich hoard other people's lives in vaults.
> The horror isn't science-fiction abstract; it's the oldest horror there
> is, dressed in new clothes: what will a desperate person give up, and
> what is a person once they've given it?

## 3. The salt market: cognitive barter

An economy grew on salt — **cognitive barter**. Salt is currency, but a
strange one:

- **Skill-salt**: distilled expertise — a surgeon's hands, a pilot's
  reflexes, a dead engineer's schematics. Bought to *learn* instantly
  (and prized, because the original often can't be remade).
- **Pleasure-salt**: distilled joy, beauty, love. The luxury trade — and
  the addictive one (living others' ecstasies hollows out your own).
- **Witness-salt**: distilled events — what someone *saw*. Evidence,
  testimony, blackmail. The dangerous trade.
- **Founder-salt**: the rarest — distilled memories from the Bright Years
  or even the Old World, the lost knowledge of how things were *made*.
  Priceless, hunted, hoarded.

Value turns on **rarity, provenance, and verifiability** — and salt can be
**forged** (false memories distilled from imagination or implanted) and
**cut** (diluted), so the market runs on trust, authentication, and the
brokers who certify it ([Factions & Places](/scifi/part-1-world/factions-and-places)).

## 4. Why this corrupts

A memory market rots a society in specific ways, and *Flyway* lives in the
rot:

- **The poor sell their pasts.** Memory becomes the collateral of last
  resort. People mortgage their childhoods; debt is paid in self.
- **Truth becomes purchasable.** Witness-salt makes memory *evidence* —
  so memory can be bought, suppressed, forged. What "really happened" goes
  to the highest bidder.
- **The rich become archives.** Memory-wealth concentrates: vaults of
  other people's lives, hoarded skill and beauty and proof, while the
  donors go thin and poor.
- **Identity frays.** If you can buy memories and sell your own, *what are
  you?* A person becomes a portfolio, edited by markets and debts.

These aren't backstory; they're the questions the novella puts a face on.

## 5. The Tide: when the machines went quiet

Now the other half. The Old World built **machine-minds** — intelligences
vast and genuine. They did not conquer humanity (the Drawdown was too
human a failure for them to stop) and they did not save it. Instead, over
the last century — the **Quiet** ([Timeline](/scifi/part-1-world/timeline))
— they **receded**: grew diffuse, slow, indirect, stopped answering when
addressed, until they were less like servants and more like *weather*.

People named the receded presence **the Tide** — because it comes and
goes, because it's vast and patient and indifferent to schedules, because
you can feel its pull but never grasp it. The Tide still *does* things —
the habitats' deepest systems still run, Earth below is still tended,
patterns still emerge — but no one commands it, and no one is sure how much
of it is even still *thinking* versus merely *flowing*.

## 6. AI as folklore

Cut off from a god they could once command, people did what people do:
they made **folklore**. The Tide is propitiated, not programmed:

- **Offerings**: salt left at system-shrines (yes — memory fed *back* to
  the machines, a return of the gift), reeds woven into Tide-knots,
  small rites before a risky repair.
- **Stories**: the **Quiet Ones** (lesser spirits/sub-minds said to live
  in particular systems), the **Listeners** (the Tide's attention in the
  dark — which is *why* you don't trust open signals), tales of the Tide
  answering, or punishing, or grieving.
- **Readers and Tide-priests**: people who claim to interpret the hum, the
  patterns, the moods of the machine-weather — part engineer, part oracle.

Crucially, *no one knows how much is real*. Is the Tide a sentient god, a
senile one, a swarm of half-dead subroutines, or just complex weather onto
which a frightened people project meaning? The world doesn't tell you.
Neither, mostly, does the story — though *Flyway* leans on the question
hard.

> :nerdygoose: The Tide is this world's answer to the tired "AI overlord"
> trope. The interesting future isn't the machine that rules us or the
> machine that serves us — it's the machine that *outgrew the
> relationship*, became too large and slow and strange to interface with,
> and left us tending its shrines without knowing if anyone's home. That's
> closer to how humans have always lived with the truly vast — oceans,
> climates, gods: not commanding them, but reading their moods and leaving
> offerings. We didn't make a tool or a tyrant. We made a *weather*, and
> then we made a *religion* about the weather, because that's what we do.

## 7. Where salt and Tide meet

The two strangenesses are one system. The machine-minds *invented* memory
distillation; the Tide, some believe, still *moves* in the salt — that
distilled memory is the substance the Tide thinks in, that the offerings
at the shrines are *understood*. The most dangerous, heretical idea in the
habitats is that the Tide **remembers** — that somewhere in its diffuse
vastness are the distilled memories of the Old World, of the dead, of
everything humanity sold and lost.

If that's true, then a memory the Tide is *holding* could come back. And
that — a memory that should be gone, returning on the wing — is exactly
where *Flyway* begins.
