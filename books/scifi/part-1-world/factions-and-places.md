# Factions & Places

> The powers and the shores. Who runs the memory markets, who tends the
> geese, who reads the Tide — and the two habitats *Flyway* moves between:
> the orbital wetland **Saltmere** and the megastructure reef **Cinder
> Reef**. This dossier maps the board the story is played on.

## 1. The Concord of Brokers

The dominant power of the Quiet is the **Concord** — the cartel of
memory-brokers who run, certify, and ultimately *control* the salt market
([Memory & Salt](/scifi/part-1-world/memory-and-salt)). The Concord arose
in the Souring ([Timeline](/scifi/part-1-world/timeline)) and now spans the
habitats: it sets prices, authenticates salt (its seal is trust itself),
settles disputes, and quietly owns the debts — which is to say, the
*pasts* — of half the population.

The Concord is not cartoonishly evil; it's *infrastructural*, the way a
central bank is. It provides real services (verification, exchange,
security) and real injustice (it owns the poor's memories, suppresses
inconvenient witness-salt, hoards founder-salt). It fears two things: salt
it cannot **verify**, and salt it cannot **control**. A memory carried by a
goose, from no broker, of something that should not exist, is both. The
Concord is the story's principal antagonist — not a villain, but a system
defending itself.

## 2. The Wardens' Guild

Older and poorer than the Concord, the **Wardens' Guild** tends the
Greywing swarms and keeps the Flyway ([The Greywings](/scifi/part-1-world/the-greywings)).
The Guild predates the Souring — it's a Bright Years institution — and it
holds a strange, stubborn power: the Concord *needs* the geese, and the
geese answer only to their wardens.

The Guild is proud, insular, ritualistic, and increasingly squeezed —
dependent on the Concord's commerce yet despising its values, guardians of
a sacred trust in a world that's turned everything to salt. Many wardens
quietly believe the heresies about the geese and the Tide
([The Greywings](/scifi/part-1-world/the-greywings)). The Guild is where
Issa Marsh belongs, and its tension with the Concord — service versus
soul — is the political spine of the story.

## 3. The Tide-priests (the Readers)

Neither faction exactly, the **Readers** (or **Tide-priests**) are those
who claim to interpret the Tide ([Memory & Salt](/scifi/part-1-world/memory-and-salt))
— its hum, its moods, its silences. They range from charlatans to genuine
mystics to engineers who simply understand the old systems better than
anyone and dress that understanding in ritual.

The Readers are tolerated, sometimes revered, sometimes feared. They tend
the system-shrines, receive the salt-offerings, and pronounce on omens —
and an out-of-season goose carrying a memory that smells of the Tide is the
most important omen in living memory. Some Readers will want to *worship*
it, some to *silence* it, some to *understand* it. They are the
wildcard faction: no one is sure whose side they're on, possibly including
themselves.

## 4. The salvagers and the dark-folk

In the margins live the **salvagers** — crews who work the dead
megastructures and the dark between habitats, scavenging Old World
material, reading the corridors, occasionally pirating a swarm (taboo, but
the desperate dare it). They're the habitats' frontier folk: rough,
independent, superstitious about the Tide (they live *in* the dark it
inhabits), and connected to everything because they go everywhere.

Salvagers are how information and contraband move *outside* the Concord's
control — and how a warden chasing a deniable secret might travel between
habitats without leaving a Concord paper-trail. They're the story's wild
country and its irregular allies.

## 5. Saltmere — the orbital wetland

**Saltmere** is home: an **orbital wetland** ([The Setting](/scifi/part-1-world/the-setting)),
a slow-turning torus of marsh and managed weather, reed-beds and water and
a soft false sky. It's beautiful and a little melancholy — humanity's
deliberate reconstruction of the wetlands it grieved, down to the mud and
the birdsong.

Saltmere is a **warden habitat**: a major Flyway waypoint, home to several
swarms and the Guild-hall, its rhythms set by the geese's seasons. It's
also poor and proud, resentful of Concord money and dependent on it. The
marsh is where the swarms nest and the wardens work; the **Guild-hall**
sits at the hub; a Concord exchange-house squats near the docks like a
polite occupation. Issa Marsh has never left it. The story will make her.

## 6. Cinder Reef — the megastructure reef

**Cinder Reef** is the destination and the dark mirror: a **megastructure
reef** ([The Setting](/scifi/part-1-world/the-setting)), the colonized hulk
of a half-built Old World ring, layer upon layer of salvage and habitation
grown into dead megastructure like coral into a wreck. Where Saltmere is
green and managed, Cinder Reef is rust and ingenuity, vertical and
crowded, lit by salvaged light.

Cinder Reef is the Concord's **stronghold** — its great exchange, its
vaults, its power. It's also where the salvagers dock, where the Readers
keep their deepest shrine (built into a still-thrumming Old World core
where the Tide is said to run strong), and where a secret goes to be
either sold or silenced. The journey from Saltmere to Cinder Reef — marsh
to reef, soul to market — is the geography of the novella's descent.

> :nerdygoose: The two habitats are built as a deliberate **diptych**.
> Saltmere is *life rebuilt* — water, growth, instinct, the goose's green
> home; Cinder Reef is *the machine inherited* — rust, salvage, market, the
> Tide's loud heart. The story's movement from one to the other is its
> thematic movement: from the warden's world of living trust to the
> broker's world of distilled, tradeable, *dead* memory — and the
> question of whether anything living can survive the crossing. Geography
> is argument here. Where a character *is* tells you which idea is winning.

## 7. The board, assembled

Put the pieces together and the conflict is structural:

- The **Concord** wants all salt verified and controlled — and fears the
  goose's uncontrollable secret.
- The **Wardens' Guild** holds the geese and a sacred trust — and is
  squeezed between service and soul.
- The **Readers** want to interpret (or worship, or silence) the Tide's
  return.
- The **salvagers** move outside all of it — the irregular path between
  habitats.
- **Saltmere** (life) and **Cinder Reef** (market) are the two poles the
  story crosses between.

Drop a single out-of-season goose, carrying a memory that should not
exist, into the middle of that board, and everyone reaches for it at once.
That's the story.
