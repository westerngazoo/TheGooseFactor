# The Setting

> Three centuries after the Drawdown, Earth is a quiet, healing ruin and
> humanity lives at the edges — in orbital wetlands and the colonized
> shells of dead megastructures. It is not an apocalypse. It is *after*
> one, long after, and people have made homes, jobs, and gods out of the
> wreckage. This is the shape of that world.

Most science fiction asks *what if the catastrophe comes?* **Flyway**
asks the quieter question: *what is it like to live, ordinarily, a long
time after one already did?* The disaster is history here — taught to
children, half-mythologized, no longer urgent. What matters is the world
that grew in its wake, and the people who think of it simply as *home*.

## 1. After, not during

The defining choice of this setting: the collapse is **over**. Three
hundred years ago, in the era people now call the **Drawdown**
([Timeline](/scifi/part-1-world/timeline)), Earth's old high civilization
came apart — not in fire, but in a slow, compounding failure of climate,
systems, and nerve. Billions did not die in a day. They died across
decades, and the survivors *left* — up the gravity well, out to the
habitats, in waves of desperate, brilliant improvisation.

By the time of *Flyway*, that's ancient history. Nobody alive remembers
Earth-as-it-was. The catastrophe is a *story*, told around recyclers and
in salt-halls. The world is not ending; it has *ended and continued*, the
way a forest continues after a fire — changed, scarred, and very much
alive.

## 2. Where people live: the edge

Humanity lives in **edge habitats** — none of them on Earth's surface,
which is left to heal (and watched over, in ways no one fully understands,
by the Tide). Two kinds dominate
([Factions & Places](/scifi/part-1-world/factions-and-places)):

- **Orbital wetlands**: engineered marsh-rings in high orbit — vast,
  slow-turning toruses of water, reed, mud, and managed weather. They are
  beautiful and deliberately *ordinary*: humanity, having lost a planet,
  rebuilt the one ecosystem it most missed. Saltmere, where our story
  begins, is one.
- **Megastructure reefs**: the half-built, abandoned orbital
  megastructures of the old world — rings, tethers, mirror-farms — now
  colonized like coral reefs, layer on layer of salvage and habitation
  grown into the dead bones. Cinder Reef is one of the largest.

Earth hangs below, green again and forbidden, a blue grave everyone can
see and no one visits.

## 3. The texture of daily life

This is a world of **salvage, ritual, and care**. There is high technology
— memory distillation, bio-synthesis, the silent machine-minds — but it is
old technology, inherited and tended rather than freshly invented, the way
we inherit cathedrals. People are not gleaming futurists; they are
*custodians* of a brilliance they only partly understand, keeping it
running with ritual as much as engineering.

Days are full of ordinary things: tending reed-beds, repairing seals,
arguing prices, raising children, burying the dead in the marsh. The
strangeness — memories sold like grain, geese carrying secrets across the
dark, offerings left to a machine-god — is *background*, as unremarkable to
them as traffic is to us. That ordinariness is the point: the future is
always someone's mundane present.

> :nerdygoose: A worldbuilding principle this setting takes seriously:
> *advanced technology, insufficiently maintained, becomes
> indistinguishable from nature and from religion.* The habitats can't
> rebuild the old machine-minds; they can only keep them fed and leave
> offerings and hope. So the line between engineer and priest has blurred.
> A flyway-warden tends the geese with checklists *and* with small rites,
> and couldn't always tell you which part is doing the work. This is what
> "techno-mythic" means here — not magic, but knowledge worn down by time
> into something that *feels* like magic to those who keep it.

## 4. The four strangenesses

Four things make this world *not ours*, and the rest of Part I details
each:

- **Memory is a substance.** It can be pulled from a mind, distilled into
  **salt**, and traded ([Memory & Salt](/scifi/part-1-world/memory-and-salt)).
  An entire economy — cognitive barter — runs on it.
- **The machines became myth.** The great intelligences receded into a
  diffuse, half-sensed presence: **the Tide**. People don't command it;
  they propitiate it ([Memory & Salt](/scifi/part-1-world/memory-and-salt)).
- **The geese carry the world.** Bio-synthetic swarms, the **Greywings**,
  migrate between habitats carrying memory and message, alive and
  therefore trustworthy ([The Greywings](/scifi/part-1-world/the-greywings)).
- **The dark is full of listeners.** Open signals between habitats are
  assumed compromised — by rivals, by salvagers, by the Tide itself —
  which is *why* the living couriers matter.

Hold these four and the world snaps into focus. They're not decoration;
they're the gears the story turns on.

## 5. What this world is about

Underneath the marsh and the salvage, *Flyway* is about **memory and
trust** — what we owe to what we remember, and who we let carry it for us.

A society that can *sell* memory has to decide what a person *is* when
their past is for sale. A society that entrusts its secrets to *living
birds* has chosen biology over its own brilliant, treacherous machines —
a choice about trust. And a society that turned its machine-gods into
*weather* has decided how to live with a power it can neither control nor
ignore. These are the questions; the geese are how the world answers them
in the only currency it trusts.

> :happygoose: Don't mistake the quiet for emptiness. This is a *lived-in*
> future — closer to a working harbor than a gleaming spire. There's mud
> under everyone's nails and salt in everyone's debts. The wonder is real,
> but it's the wonder of a place where people have made do, made homes,
> and made meaning, three hundred years after the world they were built
> for stopped existing. If that sounds melancholy, it is — but it's the
> melancholy of a marsh at dawn, full of birds and beginning again.
