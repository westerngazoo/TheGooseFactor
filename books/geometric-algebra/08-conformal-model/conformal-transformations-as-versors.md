# Conformal Versors — Every Motion Is a Sandwich

This is the promise the whole part was building to. In standard GA,
translation refused to be a sandwich — the one ugly exception to "every
transformation is a versor." In CGA that exception **vanishes**:
translations, rotations, *and* dilations and inversions — the entire
conformal group — are versors, applied by the same sandwich $V X \tilde V$
you've used since the rotors section. And it applies to *everything* —
points, lines, planes, circles, spheres — identically.

## Translation, finally a versor

The headline. A translation by $\mathbf{t}$ is the versor

$$
\boxed{\,T_\mathbf{t} = \exp\!\big(\!-\tfrac12\,\mathbf{t}\,n_\infty\big)
= 1 - \tfrac12\,\mathbf{t}\,n_\infty\,}
$$

and it acts by sandwiching: $X \mapsto T_\mathbf{t}\, X\, \tilde T_\mathbf{t}$.
Why does the exponential **truncate** to just $1 - \tfrac12\mathbf{t}
n_\infty$? Because the generating bivector $\mathbf{t}\,n_\infty$ is
**null** — it squares to zero (since $n_\infty^2 = 0$ and $\mathbf{t}$ is
orthogonal to $n_\infty$). And $\exp$ of something that squares to zero
stops after the linear term ($e^B = 1 + B$ when $B^2 = 0$). Translation is
"flat," so its versor is *linear*.

Apply it to a point $P = n_o + \mathbf{x} + \tfrac12\mathbf{x}^2 n_\infty$
and out comes — after the algebra settles — the point at $\mathbf{x} +
\mathbf{t}$, exactly. Sliding is a sandwich. The blemish on GA's "every
motion is a versor" is gone.

> :happygoose: I'm not going to pretend this didn't make me grin. The *one*
> thing standard GA couldn't do as a sandwich — translate — falls into line
> the instant you add the conformal dimensions, and it does so with the
> *same* "null generator, so $\exp$ truncates" trick that makes it so
> clean. The framework that promised "all transformations are versors" and
> then sheepishly excluded the simplest motion of all... keeps its promise
> after all. CGA is GA finishing the sentence it started.

## Rotations and rigid motions

Ordinary **rotations** are unchanged — a Euclidean rotor $R = \exp(-\tfrac
\theta2 B)$ from the rotors section is *already* a CGA versor (it lives in
the Euclidean part and fixes $n_o, n_\infty$). So a **rigid body motion** —
rotate then translate — is just the product

$$
M = T_\mathbf{t}\, R,
$$

a single versor (a **motor**, the same object the projective and applied
sections use), applied by one sandwich. Compose motions by multiplying
versors; invert by reversing. The conformal model thus contains all of
rigid-body kinematics as a special case — rotations and translations,
unified as versors and composed by the geometric product.

## Dilation and inversion — beyond rigid

CGA's real reach is *past* rigid motion, to the rest of the conformal group:

- **Dilation** (uniform scaling about the origin by factor $e^\alpha$) is
  the versor $D = \exp\!\big(\tfrac\alpha2\, n_o \wedge n_\infty\big)$. The
  bivector $n_o\wedge n_\infty$ generates scaling — it stretches the cone
  radially. Scaling, a *sandwich*.
- **Inversion** (turn space inside-out through the unit sphere,
  $\mathbf{x} \mapsto \mathbf{x}/|\mathbf{x}|^2$) is sandwiching by a
  sphere — in the null basis, essentially swapping $n_o \leftrightarrow
  n_\infty$. Inversion, the strangest classical map, is a *reflection in a
  sphere* — a sandwich by a grade-1 versor, exactly like a reflection in a
  plane.
- **Special conformal transformations** are inversion–translation–inversion
  sandwiches.

Translations, rotations, dilations, inversions — and any composition — are
all versors. That's the **entire conformal group** as sandwiches, which is
the defining feature of the model and the source of its name.

> :nerdygoose: Two facts snap together here. First, **every conformal map
> is a versor**, so the conformal group is *generated by reflections* (in
> planes and spheres) — the Cartan–Dieudonné theorem, lifted to conformal
> geometry. Second, **versors transform objects covariantly**: to move a
> sphere $S$ by a versor $V$, you sandwich it, $V S \tilde V$ — you do
> *not* re-derive the sphere from moved points. So if you've built a circle
> as $A\wedge B\wedge C$ and you translate the scene, the circle moves
> *correctly* under the same sandwich, because the wedge and the sandwich
> commute through the versor. Construct objects from points, transform with
> versors, and the algebra keeps everything consistent for free. That
> coherence — build by wedge, move by sandwich, intersect by meet, all
> interoperating — is why CGA is a *complete* geometric calculus, not just
> a notation.

## The covariance that makes it usable

The practical jewel: **transformations and constructions interoperate
seamlessly.** Whatever you built by wedging — a point-pair, circle, sphere,
line — transforms *correctly* by sandwiching with a versor, because the
versor passes through the wedge:

$$
V (A \wedge B \wedge C) \tilde V = (VA\tilde V) \wedge (VB\tilde V) \wedge (VC\tilde V).
$$

Translate the three points and you translate their circle; rotate the scene
and every sphere, line, and point-pair rotates consistently — all by the
*same* sandwich. No object-specific transform rules, no re-deriving
primitives after a motion. Construct with $\wedge$, transform with $V(\cdot)\tilde V$,
intersect with $\vee$, and the three operations cohere. *That* is why
robotics, computer vision, and CAD adopt CGA: it's a single, consistent
calculus for located, sized, curved geometry under motion.

## Closing the part

CGA completes the geometric picture standard GA started:

- **Translation is a versor** at last: $T = \exp(-\tfrac12\mathbf{t}
  n_\infty) = 1 - \tfrac12\mathbf{t} n_\infty$ (null generator → truncated
  $\exp$).
- **Rigid motions** are motors $M = T R$ — rotation and translation as one
  versor.
- **Dilations** ($n_o\wedge n_\infty$ generator) and **inversions**
  (sandwich by a sphere) extend to the **entire conformal group** — all
  versors.
- **Covariance**: objects built by $\wedge$ transform by the *same*
  sandwich $V(\cdot)\tilde V$, so construction, motion, and intersection
  interoperate.

And that closes the Conformal Model. Look at what GA now does: located
points, off-origin flats, spheres and circles, intersections, *and* the
full conformal group — all as one algebra of blades and versors. The
origin-bound blind spot is gone. Standard GA gave us directions and
rotations; CGA gives us *all* of metric geometry, motion included. The next
part takes the *other* great signature — one minus sign for time — and
finds **spacetime** and relativity waiting inside the same framework.
