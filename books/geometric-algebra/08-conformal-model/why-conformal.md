# Why Conformal — Standard GA's Blind Spot

Standard GA — the $Cl(n,0)$ we've used all along — is gorgeous for
directions, planes, and rotations. But try to do *actual geometry* with it
and you hit a wall: everything is tied to the **origin**. There's no clean
"point at $(3, 2, 1)$," no plane that doesn't pass through $\mathbf{0}$, no
sphere as a first-class object, and — most damning — **translations aren't
versors**. The **Conformal Model** (CGA) fixes all of it, by the audacious
move of adding *two* extra dimensions.

> :angrygoose: We spent five sections celebrating how GA unifies geometry,
> and there's been a polite lie underneath: standard GA can only talk about
> subspaces *through the origin*. A "line" is a direction, not a line *in
> space*. A "plane" passes through $\mathbf{0}$ by decree. Want the point
> $(3,2,1)$? Tough — you get the *vector* $3\mathbf{e}_1 + 2\mathbf{e}_2 +
> \mathbf{e}_3$, which is a direction, not a location. And translation, the
> most basic motion there is, can't be written as a sandwich. CGA is the
> apology.

## What standard GA can't do

Be specific about the blind spot. In $Cl(3,0)$:

- **No located points.** A vector $\mathbf{x}$ is a *direction and
  magnitude*, not a *position*. "The point at $\mathbf{x}$" and "the
  direction $\mathbf{x}$" are the same object — you can't tell a point from
  a vector.
- **No off-origin flats.** Every blade represents a subspace *through the
  origin*. The $xy$-plane, yes; the plane $z = 5$, no.
- **No rounds.** Spheres and circles aren't blades at all — they're not
  linear subspaces, so the algebra has no name for them.
- **Translations aren't versors.** Rotations are sandwiches $R\mathbf{x}
  \tilde R$; reflections are sandwiches. But translating $\mathbf{x} \mapsto
  \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}$ is *additive*, not a sandwich — it sits outside
  the sandwich framework that makes GA so clean.

That last one stings most. The whole appeal of GA was "every
transformation is a sandwich by a versor." Standard GA delivers that for
the *origin-fixing* transformations (the orthogonal group) but drops the
ball on the one motion a child understands: sliding something over.

## The idea: lift into a bigger space

The fix is a beautiful piece of mathematical judo. Instead of working in
$\mathbb{R}^n$, **embed it in a space with two extra dimensions** —
$\mathbb{R}^{n+1,1}$, signature $Cl(n+1, 1)$: $n$ Euclidean directions, one
extra that squares to $+1$, and one that squares to $-1$. Euclidean points
become special vectors in that bigger algebra, sitting on a **null cone**
(the next chapter), and *all* of Euclidean (indeed conformal) geometry
becomes linear algebra up there.

The payoff for two extra dimensions:

- **Points get locations** — a point at $\mathbf{x}$ is a specific *null
  vector*, distinct from a direction.
- **Off-origin flats and rounds become blades** — spheres, planes, circles,
  lines, point-pairs are all just blades of various grades.
- **Translations become versors** — sliding is finally a sandwich, joining
  rotations in the versor framework.
- **Even *inversions and dilations* become versors** — the full *conformal
  group* (angle-preserving maps) is generated by sandwiches.

You pay two dimensions and you buy the whole of metric geometry as algebra.

> :surprisedgoose: This "add dimensions to linearize" trick isn't unique to
> GA — it's the same move as **homogeneous coordinates** in graphics (add a
> $w$ to make translation a matrix multiply) and **projective space** in
> classical geometry. CGA is the *metric* version: add *two* dimensions
> (one timelike), and not just translations but *all* angle-preserving
> transformations — rotations, translations, scalings, inversions — become
> linear (versors) in the lifted space. The genius is that the lift is
> chosen so the **inner product up there encodes Euclidean distance down
> here**. You move to a stranger space precisely so the geometry you cared
> about becomes trivial there.

## Conformal = angle-preserving

The "C" is for **conformal**: angle-preserving. The transformations CGA
linearizes are exactly the **conformal maps** of Euclidean space —
rotations, translations, uniform scalings (dilations), reflections, and
inversions (the "turn-inside-out about a sphere" maps). These are the
transformations that preserve angles but not necessarily sizes or
straightness, and together they form the **conformal group**. A famous
theorem (Liouville) says in $\ge 3$ dimensions that's *all* the conformal
maps — and CGA represents every one of them as a versor. So the model is
not arbitrary: it's the natural home for the entire conformal group.

## Where this is going

This part builds CGA in four steps:

- **This chapter** — *why*: standard GA's origin-bound blind spot, and the
  promise of the conformal lift.
- **The null cone** — *how points work*: Euclidean points as null vectors,
  and the inner product that secretly computes distance.
- **Rounds and flats** — *objects as blades*: spheres, circles, planes,
  lines, point-pairs, all as wedges of points.
- **Conformal versors** — *transformations*: translations, dilations,
  inversions, and the rest, finally all sandwiches.

It's the most powerful model in applied GA — the one robotics, computer
vision, and graphics reach for — precisely because it makes *located*,
*sized*, *curved* geometry as algebraic as directions and rotations were in
the earlier sections.

> :weightliftinggoose: The motivation to carry into the next chapters:
> standard GA is **origin-bound** — no located points, no off-origin flats,
> no spheres, and (the dealbreaker) **no translations as versors**. CGA
> buys all of that by **lifting $\mathbb{R}^n$ into $\mathbb{R}^{n+1,1}$**
> (two extra dimensions, one of them squaring to $-1$), where Euclidean
> points live on a **null cone** and the inner product computes distance.
> It's the metric cousin of homogeneous coordinates, and it linearizes the
> entire **conformal group**. Two dimensions for all of metric geometry —
> the best trade in the book.

## Closing the section

The honest accounting of standard GA, and the cure:

- $Cl(n,0)$ is **origin-bound**: no located points, no off-origin
  planes/lines, no spheres/circles, and **translations aren't versors**.
- The **conformal model** lifts $\mathbb{R}^n$ into $\mathbb{R}^{n+1,1}$
  (signature $Cl(n+1,1)$), turning located/curved geometry into linear
  algebra.
- This linearizes the entire **conformal group** (rotation, translation,
  dilation, inversion) as **versors** — the sandwich framework, finally
  complete.
