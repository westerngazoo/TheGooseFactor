## What it covers

A direct comparison. The cross product:

- Only works in 3D.
- Returns a vector that's actually the *normal* to a plane (a
  hidden duality).
- Has the right-hand rule, which is an arbitrary orientation
  convention, not a derivable result.
- Doesn't associate.

The wedge product:

- Works in any dimension.
- Returns the bivector itself — the plane, with orientation.
- Orientation comes from the algebra, not from a hand convention.
- Associates.

Mathoma's framing: the cross product is a wedge product followed
by a duality, restricted to 3D, with the bivector information
thrown away. We taught the lossy version because it gave us a
3-vector output and 3-vector outputs were familiar.

## The big picture

This is the closing episode of the playlist for a reason. By the
time you've watched the first 8, you have the algebra. The 9th
video is the *philosophical* payoff: GA isn't a bigger toolbox —
it's a *cleaner* toolbox. The cross product was always a special
case dressed up as a primitive.

> :angrygoose: The cross product was Gibbs's marketing victory
> over Grassmann's wedge in the 1880s. Grassmann was right; Gibbs
> won. The cost is 140 years of confused students wondering why
> the cross product only works in 3D. The wedge has been waiting
> the whole time.
