## What it covers

The outer (wedge) product as a primitive. Antisymmetric: $\mathbf{a}\wedge\mathbf{a} = 0$.
Bilinear. Result is a *bivector* — an oriented area, not a vector.

Mathoma walks through the geometric meaning (parallelogram area
with a circulation sense), the algebraic properties, and why the
wedge of two parallel vectors is zero.

## A moment of clarity

The wedge product is what the cross product *should have been*.
The video makes the comparison explicit; the book does the same
in [Episode 9's chapter](/geometric-algebra/02-products-and-operations/the-outer-product)
(wedge vs. cross). If you've ever found the cross product
arbitrary — "why does it only work in 3D?" — the wedge is the
answer.

> :angrygoose: We teach the cross product first because it
> happens to give a vector and vectors are familiar. The wedge
> gives a bivector and bivectors are new. So we choose the
> dimension-locked accident over the universal truth. Pedagogy
> by familiarity, not by depth.
